01 — Overview & Problem Statement

What Are We Solving?

Urban infrastructure — bridges, flyovers, building columns, retaining walls, and elevated transit structures — degrades invisibly. Material fatigue, micro-cracking, and joint loosening progress silently over months and years, long before any surface-level visual symptom appears. By the time a crack is visible, the structural damage is already severe.

Currently, cities rely on periodic manual inspection — an inspector walks the structure, looks at it, and files a report. In India, this happens once every 2–5 years for most civic infrastructure. There is no real-time, continuous, or affordable monitoring system deployed at scale in any Indian city.

The core insight: Structural failure has a distinct acoustic and vibrational signature. A healthy steel beam resonates at a predictable frequency under load. A beam with micro-cracks or loose joints resonates differently — the frequency shifts, harmonics appear, and damping characteristics change. This is measurable with a ₹200 sensor. Nobody is measuring it.

Urban Development Relevance

As Indian cities expand rapidly — new flyovers, metro pillars, pedestrian bridges, elevated roads — the volume of infrastructure to monitor grows faster than inspection capacity. UrbanPulse is not a research curiosity; it is the missing layer of the smart city stack. Every smart city initiative currently has traffic sensors, air quality sensors, and waste sensors. None have structural health sensors. This is the gap.

02 — Objectives

What We Are Building

Primary Objective

Design, build, and demonstrate a low-cost, wireless, multi-node Structural Health Monitoring (SHM) system that detects anomalous vibration signatures in structural members using a combination of MEMS accelerometers and piezoelectric sensors, processes data via FFT and ML anomaly detection, and presents real-time health indices on a web dashboard.

Secondary Objectives

1. Multi-node wireless mesh: At least 3 ESP32 sensor nodes communicating over WiFi mesh (ESP-NOW protocol), each independently sampling and transmitting structural data without a central wired hub.

2. FFT-based frequency analysis: Each node performs on-device Fast Fourier Transform to extract dominant frequency components and detect resonance shifts in real time.

3. ML anomaly detection: A backend model (Isolation Forest for rapid prototyping, upgradeable to LSTM autoencoder for paper) flags statistically anomalous vibration events with severity classification: Normal → Warning → Critical.

4. Physical tabletop prototype: A steel frame structure (simulating a bridge truss or building column) with sensor nodes clamped at key structural points, demonstrable under induced stress during presentation.

5. Real-time web dashboard: A React frontend showing per-node health scores, live FFT waveforms, alert history, and a structural map of the demo model — accessible from any device on the same network.

6. Alert system: Automatic buzzer + LED alert on the physical prototype when a Critical event is detected, plus a web notification push.

Stretch Objectives (if time permits)

Battery + solar power for each node making them fully wireless and self-sustaining. LSTM autoencoder replacing Isolation Forest for the paper. Historical trend visualisation showing structural degradation over weeks of data collection.

03 — Innovation & Differentiation

What Makes This New?

📡
Sensor Fusion SHM
Combining MEMS accelerometer (low-frequency structural sway) with piezoelectric sensor (high-frequency stress waves / acoustic emission) gives a full-spectrum vibration profile. Neither sensor alone is sufficient — the fusion is the novelty.
⚡
On-Device FFT
FFT runs on the ESP32 itself before transmission, not on a server. This means only frequency-domain features (not raw 10kHz sample streams) travel over WiFi — reducing bandwidth by ~98% and making the system scalable to hundreds of nodes.
🧠
Unsupervised Anomaly ML
No labelled failure dataset exists for most structures. The Isolation Forest model trains on healthy baseline data collected in the first week, then flags deviations — making the system deployable on any structure without prior failure history.
🔗
ESP-NOW Mesh (No Router)
Nodes communicate peer-to-peer via ESP-NOW — no WiFi router or internet required at the structure. A single gateway node then pushes to the cloud. This makes deployment on remote bridges or infrastructure in low-connectivity areas viable.
💰
₹500/node Cost Point
Existing commercial SHM systems (from companies like Trimble or National Instruments) cost ₹50,000–₹5,00,000 per sensor point. UrbanPulse targets ₹500 per node — a 100× cost reduction that makes city-scale deployment economically feasible.
🏙️
Urban Development Gap
Smart city sensor deployments globally include air, traffic, waste, and water. Structural health monitoring at the urban scale does not exist in any Indian smart city deployment today. This is a first-mover position, not an incremental improvement.
04 — System Architecture

How It All Works

Full Pipeline — Layer by Layer

Layer 1
Physical Sensing
MPU-6050 (I²C) samples 3-axis acceleration at 1kHz. Piezoelectric disc (analog) samples acoustic emission at up to 10kHz via ESP32 ADC. Both run simultaneously per node. Nodes are clamped/epoxied to structural members at joints, midspan, and support points — the highest-stress locations based on structural mechanics.
Layer 2
Edge Processing
On-device FFT (Arduino FFT library or esp-dsp) converts 512-sample windows into frequency spectra every 500ms. Dominant frequency, peak amplitude, and spectral centroid are extracted per window. Raw samples are discarded. Only these 3 features per window per sensor are transmitted — lightweight and fast.
Layer 3
Wireless Mesh
ESP-NOW peer-to-peer protocol. Nodes 2 and 3 transmit to Node 1 (gateway) directly — no router needed at the structure. Node 1 (gateway) has dual-role: ESP-NOW receiver from peers + WiFi client to local network / MQTT broker. Latency under 50ms node-to-gateway.
Layer 4
Backend + ML
Python Flask / Node.js backend receives MQTT messages, stores time-series in SQLite (demo) or InfluxDB (production). Isolation Forest model trains on first 1000 healthy readings per node. Subsequent readings are scored — anomaly score above threshold triggers a Warning or Critical event. Events logged with timestamp and node ID.
Layer 5
Dashboard
React frontend with Chart.js / Recharts for live FFT waveform visualization per node, health score gauge (0–100), alert timeline, and a 2D structural map of the demo frame showing which nodes are in which state (green/amber/red). WebSocket connection for <1s update latency.
Layer 6
Physical Alert
When backend pushes a Critical event via MQTT back to gateway node, ESP32 GPIO triggers: red LED strip on the physical prototype illuminates, buzzer sounds 3 short pulses. This closes the loop — the physical model responds to its own health data. This is the demo moment.

Physical Prototype Design

Frame: A 60cm × 40cm steel angle iron or flat bar frame, welded or bolted, simulating a simple bridge truss or building column section. Available from any hardware shop for ~₹200–300.

Node placement: Node A at the left support joint. Node B at midspan (highest bending stress). Node C at the right support joint. Each node is a small PCB/breadboard box with MPU-6050, piezo disc, ESP32, and a 3.7V LiPo cell, fixed with a strong magnet or M4 bolt clamp.

Stress induction for demo: A small weight (500g) hung from midspan, or a gentle tap with a rubber mallet. This induces a measurable vibration event that the system detects within 1–2 seconds on the dashboard.

Damage simulation: Pre-loosen one bolt at a joint before the demo. The frequency shift from the loose joint is detectable by the system. This is your "cracked bridge" simulation — elegant, realistic, and physically demonstrable.

08 — Live Demo Script

60-Second Judge Walkthrough

This is your most important asset. Every step below should be rehearsed until it takes exactly the stated time. The goal: a judge who has never seen this project walks away with a complete mental picture in under 90 seconds.

01
Set the scene — 10 seconds
Point to the steel frame. "This represents a bridge section. These three nodes are continuously measuring vibration and acoustic signatures. Right now, the structure is healthy — all green on the dashboard." Judge looks at the screen, sees three green gauges.
02
Show normal operation — 15 seconds
Tap the frame gently with a finger. "Normal vibration — a person walking on the bridge. The system sees it, the FFT shows the frequency spike, but the anomaly score stays in the safe zone." Dashboard shows FFT spike, health score dips briefly and recovers.
03
Induce the fault — 10 seconds
Slightly loosen the bolt at the midspan joint (pre-prepared). "I've just simulated a micro-fracture at the joint — what you'd see in a real bridge after years of fatigue loading." Nothing visible changes on the structure. This is the point.
04
The detection moment — 15 seconds
Tap the frame at the same force as before. "Same tap. Watch." The frequency spectrum has shifted — new harmonics appear. The anomaly score crosses the threshold. Dashboard flips Node B to amber, then red. The LED strip on the frame turns red. The buzzer fires three pulses. The alert log timestamps the event.
05
The punchline — 10 seconds
"A visual inspector would not have caught that. The bolt looks fine. But the acoustic signature changed, the ML model flagged it, and we got a timestamped alert — before any visible damage occurred. This is what we're deploying on real bridges."
Critical demo reliability rules: Run the full demo at least 10 times before presentation day. The loose-bolt simulation must be calibrated — too loose and the frequency shift is obvious even without sensors; just right and only the sensor catches it. Keep a fully charged power bank on standby. Have the dashboard open on two devices (laptop + phone) in case one fails. The demo must work without internet — all backend runs locally.
